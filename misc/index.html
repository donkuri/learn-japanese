    Miscellaneous

    This section discusses miscellaneous things that did not fit in any of the other category directly.

    Certifications

    There are multiple Japanese certifications a learner might be interested in.

    Japanese Language Proficiency Test

    Perhaps the most popular Japanese language certification, the JLPT has 5 levels testing Japanese proficiency, from N5 (lowest) to N1 (highest). The N2 level is required by many universities in order to study there. The N1 level can be used to get points in order to get a visa in the country. I suggest you go straight for N1.

    Is it worth getting the JLPT for a level lower than N2?

    I would only consider doing that in two very specific cases. The first one is the most obvious, you require a certain certificate (under N2) for something. I think in this case it's very valid, but I am not sure what such a certificate would be useful for, outside of maybe Japanese courses at a university. I am not a huge fan, but you do you. The second case is for extra motivation. This one I would advise against, because motivation should come from wanting to read, listen, speak or write Japanese. If neither of these is motivating to you, why are you learning the language? This is especially true for the N4 and N5 levels.

    How should I study for the JLPT?

    The JLPT examines your basic Japanese level, thus you should be able to read and listen well. There is no output section. This means that if you can read and listen to just about anything, you can pass N1. The passing grade is very low, sitting at 100/180 for N1 and requiring at least 19 points in each section. You can and you should prioritize reading and listening through immersion, especially through a variety of immersion media. That being said, it might be interesting to grind out the SKM books before the exam, perhaps a month before. This is especially true in my opinion of the grammar books, though you should already be familiar with advanced grammar by the time you consider taking the N1.

    漢字検定

    The 漢字検定, usually referred to as Kanken is a test made for natives to test their kanji knowledge. The test has a variety of level with the easiest being 10級 and the hardest being 1級. The higher levels are noticeably hard, so much so that there's a very short list of non-natives who have passed 1級. One such person is the amazing Bret Mayer. The 2級 tests all 常用漢字, but it includes 四字熟語 as well as more obscure readings. The higher levels also include ことわざ and the like. If these terms do not mean anything to you, I suggest you skip this section for now. If these do mean anything to you, I still suggests you skip this section unless you are somehow really interested in writing kanji at a very high level, then keep reading.

    What level should I aim for?

    Up to you, but I personally don't think I'd ever aim for more than 2級, maybe 準2級. Keep in mind, you need to be able to write all of these kanji. It is not sufficient to know the reading of 魑魅魍魎, you need to be able to write it perfectly. Overall, I recommend interested learners to aim between 3級 and 2級. See this classification below:

    How should I study for Kanken?

    I am not an expert on the subject but the preferred method in the community seems to be doing this deck (both parts). Some people like to grind the 3DS game 漢検トレーニング2, usually via emulation. Another way is using books made for natives. The ナツメ社 series, 旺文社 series and Kanken's own quick study series are great if you already know how to write. The ステップ series on the other hand is better if you don't. If you go with 旺文社, consider the pocket edition. More generally, Kanken textbooks are listed here. Either way, make sure you check out this list.

    Other certifications

    I do not know much about the other kinds of certifications people can take. A famous one is the Business Japanese Proficiency Test, usually written BJT. A few years ago, the J-CAT was a pretty popular test, I am not sure if it's still well-known nowadays but it's pretty good. Finally, the 日本語検定 is a test made for natives to test their Japanese ability. If you just wanna do fun certifications in Japanese, see this.
